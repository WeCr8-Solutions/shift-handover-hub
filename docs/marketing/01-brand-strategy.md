# 01 — Brand Strategy

> **Thesis.** The brand has to land on two skeptical audiences at once — shop owners and operators — without talking down to either. Voice is shop-floor credible, plain-spoken, confident, and wry. Founder is the brand for two years. The brand-debt rules at the bottom of this doc override any short-term growth tactic that conflicts with them, including anything in 02–10.


## Brand promise

**We make shop-floor work visible, portable, and respected** — without replacing the people who do it.

That promise has to be felt by two audiences simultaneously:
- **Shops**: "These people understand my floor."
- **Operators**: "These people are on my side."

If either side reads our copy and feels talked down to or sold at, we lose them.

## Brand voice

| Trait | What it sounds like | What it doesn't |
|---|---|---|
| **Shop-floor credible** | "Tolerance," "first article," "G54," "handoff." Speak the trade. | Generic SaaS verbs ("empower," "unlock," "leverage"). |
| **Plain-spoken** | Short sentences. Direct claims with numbers. | Long abstract paragraphs. Vision-statement prose. |
| **Confident, not loud** | "It works on the floor. Try it for two shifts." | "Revolutionary. Transformational. The future of manufacturing." |
| **Respectful of operators** | Operators are professionals with hard-won skill. | "Upskill your workforce." Operators do not want to be a workforce. |
| **Wry, occasionally** | A little dry humor about whiteboards, sticky notes, and missing travelers. | Sarcasm aimed at customers' tools or processes. |

Test: if a 30-year journeyman machinist reads a sentence we wrote and rolls his eyes, rewrite it.

## Visual principles

- **Restraint over decoration.** Tools, not posters. White space is fine.
- **Real shop photography over stock.** When budget allows, commission. Until then, prefer machine close-ups, hands, tooling, parts — not smiling office workers in PPE.
- **Color**: industrial, not neon. Steel, machine-blue, safety-yellow used sparingly as an accent.
- **Type**: legible at arm's length on a shop-floor tablet. No display-weight serifs.
- **Icons**: trade-accurate. A 4-jaw chuck icon, not a generic gear.

## Narrative arc

The story we keep telling, across every surface, in this order:

1. **The expeditor problem is universal.** Every shop has one. Every shop is one person away from chaos.
2. **Software didn't solve it because software didn't show up on the floor.** ERPs live in the front office. MES costs a quarter-million. Whiteboards still win.
3. **We built the digital expeditor.** Lives on every tablet, every station, every shift.
4. **Operators get something out of it too** — credentials, a profile, a path. So they actually use it.
5. **And the data compounds.** Better handoffs → better credentials → better hiring → better shops.

Decks, talks, blog posts, and homepage hero all walk some subset of this arc. Order matters: don't lead with credentials before pain.

## Founder visibility

The founder is the brand for the first two years. Plan accordingly:

- **Be on the floor publicly.** Photos and clips of shop visits, machine work, conversations with operators. Authenticity beats production value.
- **Write under the founder's name**, not "the JobLine team." First-person essays on trade pubs, LinkedIn, and the blog.
- **Take the unpopular shop-side positions out loud** — e.g., "MES is too expensive for 95% of shops and that's fine," "operators should own their credentials." Strong opinions attract the right customers and repel the wrong ones.
- **Show up at shows in work clothes, not vendor polos.** Conference posture covered in doc 06.

## Story angles to cultivate

The press and partners will repeat the angles we hand them. Pre-package these:

- **"The expeditor in software."** Trade-press friendly. Easy headline.
- **"Operator credentials as workforce infrastructure."** Workforce/policy/grant audience.
- **"Why the shop floor never went digital."** Founder essay angle, contrarian, evergreen.
- **"What ERP got wrong about the floor."** ERP-vendor-friendly when framed as "we complete you, not replace you."
- **"The two-sided shop network."** Investor and analyst angle.

## What to avoid in the brand

- **Don't compete on UI flash.** The audience suspects pretty software of being shallow.
- **Don't claim AI as identity.** Mention it where it earns its keep (planning assistant, programming portability) and let the work speak.
- **Don't moralize.** No "the future of work" thinkpieces. Tools, not manifestos.
- **Don't punch down at the trade.** Never imply shops are "behind" or need to be "transformed." They are operating to tolerances we couldn't hit on our best day.

## Brand-debt rules

These are the things we will *not* trade away for short-term growth:

- No surveillance framing in operator-facing copy, ever.
- No ITAR-violating geographic targeting in any campaign.
- No paid placements that imply endorsement we haven't earned (e.g., logos of shops that aren't paying customers).
- No "AI" rebrand if model hype shifts. Identity stays anchored to the expeditor.
