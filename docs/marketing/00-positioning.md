# 00 — Positioning

> **Thesis.** We are creating the "Digital Expeditor" category for US precision machine shops (10–100 employees, regulated end-markets) and a credentialed talent layer on the supply side. We coexist with ERPs, we compete with whiteboards, and we never lead with "AI." Every public surface must ladder to one of three pillars: *handoff that doesn't get lost*, *credentials that travel*, *works with your ERP*. Read alongside 01 (voice) and 10 (the breakout artifact that makes this category legible to the world).


## The category we're creating

**Digital Expeditor for precision machine shops.**

An expeditor is the person on a shop floor who chases jobs across stations, knows where every part is, and keeps work moving across shifts. Every shop has one — usually a senior operator or a working supervisor. They are the single biggest source of tribal knowledge and the single biggest bottleneck when they're off the floor.

JobLine.ai *is* that expeditor, in software, available across every shift, every station, and every operator.

This framing matters because it is **immediately legible to shop owners** and immediately differentiated from MES, ERP, scheduling, and "shop floor app" categories — all of which shop owners have heard pitches for and mostly ignored.

## What we are

- A shift-handoff and job-flow system that operators actually open.
- A credentialing layer (GCA + OAP) that makes operator skill visible and portable.
- A talent network where credentialed operators have public profiles and shops can recruit.
- An optional read-through layer on top of existing ERPs (JobBOSS, SAP) — we do not replace them.

## What we are NOT

- Not an ERP. We do not want to be the system of record for quoting, AR/AP, or inventory.
- Not an MES. We don't sell machine-monitoring boxes or claim OEE as our headline.
- Not a generic LMS. GCA and OAP exist because the trade has no good credential layer — not because we want to be Coursera.
- Not a generic job board. Talent profiles are a moat for the shop product, not a standalone Indeed competitor.

## ICP — Shops (demand side)

**Primary**: US precision machining and fabrication shops, **10–100 employees**, running multiple shifts, with at least one of: AS9100, ISO 9001, ITAR registration, medical/defense end-customers.

Why this segment:
- Big enough that shift handoff is genuinely painful (single-shift shops solve it with a sticky note).
- Small enough that the owner/GM signs the contract and there is no procurement gauntlet.
- Regulated end-markets mean documentation and traceability are already non-optional, so paying for software that produces them is rational.
- Already underserved by enterprise MES (too expensive, too heavy) and overserved by generic SaaS (Trello, Slack) that don't speak shop-floor.

**Secondary**: 100–250 employee shops with multi-site operations. Higher-value contract but longer sales cycle — pursue only when warm.

**Explicitly out of ICP** for now: single-shift hobby/prototype shops, contract manufacturers > 500 employees, OEMs with internal IT building custom systems.

## ICP — Operators / Talent (supply side)

**Primary**: CNC machinists, setup operators, programmers, and QC inspectors with **2–20 years of shop-floor experience**, currently employed at an ICP shop or actively looking to move. Skews 25–55, mobile-literate but not laptop-bound, suspicious of corporate platforms.

Why this segment:
- They are the people whose tribal knowledge the digital expeditor captures.
- Their credentials (GCA, OAP, certificates) are the supply that makes the talent side valuable.
- They influence shop tool-buying decisions more than supervisors do.
- LinkedIn does not serve them well; Indeed treats them as commodity labor.

**Secondary**: trade-school students and recent graduates entering the field. Lower lifetime value short-term but they grow into the primary segment.

## Three messaging pillars

1. **"The shift handoff that doesn't get lost."** — Anchor benefit. Every shop owner has lost money to a bad handoff.
2. **"Operator credentials that travel with the operator."** — Supply-side moat. GCA + OAP make skill visible and verifiable.
3. **"Works with your ERP, not against it."** — Sales-cycle unblock. Removes the rip-and-replace objection that kills most shop-floor SaaS deals.

Every public surface (homepage, decks, ads, talks) should reduce cleanly to one of these three. If a message doesn't ladder up to one of them, cut it.

## Things to never say in copy

- "AI-powered" as the lead. We have AI; it's not the product.
- "Industry 4.0," "smart factory," "digital transformation." Owners have buzzword fatigue.
- "Enterprise-grade." Sounds expensive to a 40-person shop.
- Anything that implies we replace the ERP.
- Anything that implies operators are being surveilled.
